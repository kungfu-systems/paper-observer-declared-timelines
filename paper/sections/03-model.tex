\section{Observer-Declared Timeline Model}
\label{sec:model}

An observer-declared timeline is a deterministic projection over accepted
causal objects from a declared, versioned perspective. The model is
intentionally small; it is a surface and verification contract, not a physical
database schema.

\subsection{Facts and sources}

A \emph{fact} is an event, record, manifest, receipt, payload hash, causal
edge, or verification result that the system treats as evidence. A fact has a
source, provenance, and a local identity. A fact source may be a local runtime,
remote runtime, repository, release artifact, adapter, imported bundle, or
external system.

The view does not need to accept every fact the product has ever seen. It must
declare which source ranges, heads, cursors, manifests, watermarks, or
freshness boundaries were accepted for the current projection.

\subsection{Episodes and causal closure}

Let an Episode be

\[
E_i = (F_i, \prec_i, D_i, M_i),
\]

where $F_i$ is its fact set, $\prec_i$ is the known causal relation inside the
Episode, $D_i$ is its declared dependency set over other Episodes, and $M_i$ is
the manifest and verification evidence that defines the object. Frame-level
causal edges must close inside $E_i$. Influence from another Episode must be
represented by $D_i$, rather than by an undeclared edge that escapes the object
boundary.

For one view, the accepted set $A$ contains selected Episodes plus the source
ranges, heads, watermarks, and freshness boundaries under which they were
accepted. The induced causal relation $\prec_A$ is the union of each
$\prec_i$ and the dependency edges declared by $D_i$. If required evidence is
missing, $A$ remains inspectable but carries degraded evidence rather than a
fabricated closure claim.

\subsection{Logical observer and incarnation}

A \emph{logical observer} is the stable perspective whose view is requested.
It may represent a human, agent, runtime location, workspace, service, or
product view. It is not necessarily the author or owner of the facts.

An \emph{observer incarnation} is one evidence-bearing realization of that
logical observer. We model it as

\[
O^e = (o, e, r, v, c_a, c_p),
\]

where $o$ is the logical observer identity, $e$ is a monotonic incarnation or
epoch identity, $r$ identifies the runtime or implementation that performs the
projection, $v$ identifies the projection-policy semantics, $c_a$ is the
accepted authority cut, and $c_p$ is the cut reached by the derived projection.
The concrete representation may differ by product. The invariant is that a
process name, PID, socket, heartbeat, or GUI instance cannot substitute for
these claims.

The projection cut cannot lead the accepted authority cut. If the system cannot
show that the projection reached the declared authority, the view is stale or
recovering. If it cannot identify its semantic version, reproduction by another
incarnation is unproven.

\subsection{Continuity witness}

Replacing an incarnation creates a transition, not identity by assertion. Let

\[
W_{e \rightarrow e+1} = (O^e, O^{e+1}, c, q, R),
\]

where $c$ is the predecessor cut accepted by the successor, $q$ states the
policy/runtime compatibility relation, and $R$ is retained recovery evidence.
A valid witness shows that the successor rejected stale authority, accepted a
non-regressing cut, rebuilt or verified its derived state, and reached a
declared readiness condition. A policy change may still be continuous at the
fact layer while producing a new view identity; the witness must not erase that
semantic change.

This yields three separate continuity claims:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \emph{fact-authority continuity}: accepted facts remain intact and
        verifiable across process or software replacement;
  \item \emph{observer continuity}: a successor incarnation proves how its
        accepted cut and semantics relate to its predecessor; and
  \item \emph{presentation continuity}: a GUI, CLI, or agent interface can
        reconnect and display a coherent view.
\end{itemize}

Presentation continuity cannot prove the first two. A familiar screen may be
backed by stale authority, and a preserved terminal stream may still lack a
valid work-completion claim. Conversely, authority may remain sound while the
presentation is unavailable or explicitly recovering.

\subsection{Projection policy}

A projection policy $P_v$ orders accepted facts for display, replay, export,
or agent consumption under semantic version $v$. For incarnation $O^e$, the
visible timeline is

\[
T_{O^e,P_v} = \Pi_{O^e,P_v}(A, \prec_A).
\]

The projection $\Pi$ must be deterministic and must produce a linear extension
of every known causal constraint. If $a \prec_A b$, a valid projection cannot
place $b$ before $a$. Concurrent or causally unrelated facts may be ordered by
observer policy, source priority, local sequence, or a deterministic
tie-breaker, but that policy must be inspectable and versioned. When no valid
total order can be supported by the accepted evidence, the result is degraded
or rejected rather than silently repaired with wall-clock order.

\subsection{Authority and derived views}

The accepted facts and their manifests are authority inputs. A folded current
view, database index, GUI timeline, or exported ordering is derived. Deleting
and rebuilding a derived projection must not change authority, and disagreement
between a projection and authority must be reported as drift. Runtime
activation, recovery, and upgrade mechanisms may select or validate a
projection, but they do not acquire ownership of the underlying facts.

\subsection{Degraded evidence}

A timeline may be useful even when evidence is incomplete. Instead of hiding
that incompleteness, the view should declare degraded states such as missing
causality, incomplete accepted ranges, stale remote facts, redacted payloads,
schema mismatch, unverifiable source, unresolved conflict, unknown continuity,
or a projection that has not reached its required cut. A degraded view is
preferable to an overconfident total order.

Degradation is operation-sensitive. Missing payload bytes may prevent replay
while still allowing manifest inspection; a stale index may prevent indexed
query while still allowing rebuild; a missing continuity witness may prevent a
seamless-history claim while preserving both incarnations' local facts. A
single global health bit is therefore weaker than a report that names the
evidence gap and the safe capabilities that remain.

\subsection{Minimal surface}

The minimal observer-declared surface contains:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item a stable view identifier and view subject;
  \item logical observer identity, current incarnation, and predecessor when
        continuity is claimed;
  \item accepted fact sources, ranges, and authority cut;
  \item projection cut, policy version, causal dominance, and tie-breaker;
  \item runtime or implementation identity sufficient for reproduction;
  \item causal constraints and continuity-witness status;
  \item degraded evidence states and safe remaining operations; and
  \item verification status and evidence references.
\end{itemize}

This extends the KFD-4 observer-perspective schema published by the KFD package
\cite{kfd2026observer}. The schema is a vocabulary and verification boundary,
not a claim that an adopter's runtime implementation is correct merely because
it imports the schema. Incarnation fields are a proposed refinement derived
from implementation experience; they are not yet a published KFD-4 schema
revision.
