\section{Observer-Declared Timeline Model}
\label{sec:model}

An observer-declared timeline is a deterministic projection over accepted
causal objects from a declared perspective. The model is intentionally small;
it is a surface and verification contract, not a physical database schema.

\subsection{Facts and sources}

A \emph{fact} is an event, record, manifest, receipt, payload hash, causal
edge, or verification result that the system treats as evidence. A fact has a
source, provenance, and a local identity. A fact source may be a local runtime,
remote runtime, repository, release artifact, adapter, imported bundle, or
external system.

The view does not need to accept every fact the product has ever seen. It must
declare which source ranges, heads, cursors, manifests, watermarks, or
freshness boundaries were accepted for the current projection.

\subsection{Episodes and causal closure}

Let an Episode be

\[
E_i = (F_i, \prec_i, D_i, M_i),
\]

where $F_i$ is its fact set, $\prec_i$ is the known causal relation inside the
Episode, $D_i$ is its declared dependency set over other Episodes, and $M_i$ is
the manifest and verification evidence that defines the object. Frame-level
causal edges must close inside $E_i$. Influence from another Episode must be
represented by $D_i$, rather than by an undeclared edge that escapes the object
boundary.

For one view, the accepted set $A$ contains selected Episodes plus the source
ranges, heads, watermarks, and freshness boundaries under which they were
accepted. The induced causal relation $\prec_A$ is the union of each
$\prec_i$ and the dependency edges declared by $D_i$. If required evidence is
missing, $A$ remains inspectable but carries degraded evidence rather than a
fabricated closure claim.

\subsection{Observer}

An \emph{observer} is the perspective whose view is being shown. It may be a
human, agent, runtime location, service, or product view. The observer is not
necessarily the author of the facts. It is the subject from whose accepted
fact set and projection policy the timeline is derived.

\subsection{Projection policy}

A projection policy $P$ orders accepted facts for display, replay, export, or
agent consumption. For observer $O$, the visible timeline is

\[
T_{O,P} = \Pi_{O,P}(A, \prec_A).
\]

The projection $\Pi$ must be deterministic and must produce a linear extension
of every known causal constraint. If $a \prec_A b$, a valid projection cannot
place $b$ before $a$. Concurrent or causally unrelated facts may be ordered by
observer policy, source priority, local sequence, or a deterministic
tie-breaker, but that policy must be inspectable and versioned. When no valid
total order can be supported by the accepted evidence, the result is degraded
or rejected rather than silently repaired with wall-clock order.

\subsection{Authority and derived views}

The accepted facts and their manifests are authority inputs. A folded current
view, database index, GUI timeline, or exported ordering is derived. Deleting
and rebuilding a derived projection must not change authority, and disagreement
between a projection and authority must be reported as drift. This separation
is necessary for an observer declaration to be meaningful: an observer states
which authority facts and policy produced the view, not merely which cached rows
were displayed.

\subsection{Degraded evidence}

A timeline may be useful even when evidence is incomplete. Instead of hiding
that incompleteness, the view should declare degraded states such as missing
causality, incomplete accepted ranges, stale remote facts, redacted payloads,
schema mismatch, unverifiable source, or unresolved conflict. A degraded view
is preferable to an overconfident total order.

Degradation is operation-sensitive. Missing payload bytes may prevent replay
while still allowing manifest inspection; a stale index may prevent indexed
query while still allowing rebuild; a missing dependency may prevent a complete
projection while preserving the local Episode facts. A single global health bit
is therefore weaker than a report that names the evidence gap and the safe
capabilities that remain.

\subsection{Minimal surface}

The minimal observer-declared surface contains:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item a stable view identifier;
  \item the view subject, such as timeline, history, replay, sync, ordering,
        or mixed-source work state;
  \item observer identity and kind;
  \item accepted fact sources and ranges;
  \item projection policy version, causal dominance, and tie-breaker;
  \item causal constraints;
  \item degraded evidence states; and
  \item verification status.
\end{itemize}

This is close to the KFD-4 observer-perspective schema published by the KFD
package \cite{kfd2026observer}. The schema is a vocabulary and verification
boundary, not a claim that an adopter's runtime implementation is correct merely
because it imports the schema.
